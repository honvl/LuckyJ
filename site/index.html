Safe tiles and timing

The correction is narrower defense.

The new statistical pass sharpened a normal riichi concept: genbutsu and suji are resources, not trophies. "Keep a safe tile because it is safe" is too broad. The stronger LuckyJ rule is timed, target-specific, and connected to hand value.

Safe tiles

Safe tiles expire.

A river-safe tile loses value when it no longer defends the live danger or when keeping it steals the hand's route.

Read point 15
Shape

Late outside cuts can preserve route.

Under pressure, cutting the edge tile can be the way to keep an inner winning path without adding immediate danger.

Read point 16
Defense

Defense is target-specific.

Kept genbutsu and suji matter most when they answer a live riichi or open threat in the middle or late row.

Read point 17
Honors

Honors need role labels.

Self value, opponent contract tile, dead honor, and safe exit are different jobs; do not review them as one honor bucket.

Read point 18
Placement

Leader safety is a qualifier.

With a lead, reduced ambition is only good when the discard also avoids extra danger and preserves a clean next turn.

Read point 19
Revision to point 14: Keep safe exits when they defend the next real threat. Do not keep them as trophies.

Sources and limits

What the evidence can and cannot prove.

The NAGA reports show what LuckyJ did and where NAGA models disagreed. Mortal is used as a cross-check, not as authority. The new points are folded into normal riichi concepts: value, shape, safe tiles, opponent contracts, and placement.
