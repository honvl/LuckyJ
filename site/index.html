What this book is

A practical manual, not a score worship page.

The source data contains every LuckyJ game in the sheet that had an exportable NAGA report: 1,256 hanchan, 13,059 hands, and 147,378 non-reached LuckyJ discard decisions. I also used supporting hand-review material to interpret the patterns. The goal is not to say every LuckyJ move is correct. The goal is to identify the repeatable principles behind the results and turn them into rules you can apply at the table.

The points

The LuckyJ Operating System

LuckyJ does not play like a pure tile-efficiency chart. It uses tile efficiency as one component inside a placement engine. The recurring pattern is: define the hand's job, keep routes that satisfy that job, preserve enough defense to keep acting, and abandon stale plans immediately when the table changes.

Decision method

How to read every discard.

1. Job

Name the placement job before looking for the prettiest shape. A hand that does not change the table state is not allowed to buy much risk.

2. Routes

Count real routes, not only raw tile count: closed riichi, open yaku, value upgrade, safe tenpai, and clean fold.

3. Table Texture

Read the opponents' rivers and calls before trusting raw danger. LuckyJ often treats one open hand, one riichi, and two live threats as different games.

4. Future Danger

Price the tiles you will have to discard later. A tile that is fine now can become the discard that destroys the hand.

5. Point of No Return

Decide what draw, call, riichi, or second threat will end the attack. LuckyJ-style aggression has an exit rule.

6. Human Override

Use NAGA, Mortal, and your own late-game review to check LuckyJ's third-row choices. That is the least copyable part of the style.

Pattern synthesis

What the supporting hand reviews add.

The supporting reviews are useful because they study LuckyJ from different angles: all East 1 starts, placement-specific hands, last-place-avoidance games, and comparisons against NAGA and Mortal. I used them as commentary, not as authority. The distilled lesson is that LuckyJ's style is a set of switches: hand value, hand distance, number of calls, river texture, round, and placement all change the meaning of the same tile.

East 1 Reveals the Base Settings

With scores equal and placement pressure absent, LuckyJ's default target is not "maximum raw speed." As a child, it strongly prefers either a good-shape one-han riichi route or a hand that can become three han or better. As dealer, the value threshold drops because the dealer turn is worth more. Hands below those thresholds start preserving yaku, dora, and safety.

Hold Safety by Shanten Count

The farther the hand is, the more a safe tile is worth. A far-away hand can keep honors or visible safe material without "giving up," because those tiles buy permission to call, push one more tile, or reach safe tenpai later. Dealer status and very high-value hands lower that safety premium.

Weak Penchans Are Not Sacred Blocks

LuckyJ is willing to break a penchan or bad block when its best future is riichi-only bad shape. The kept tiles usually have another job: yakuhai pair, honitsu/chanta route, dora use, or defense. Do not copy this as vague slimming. Copy it only when the bad block mainly creates a hand you do not want to win.

Read the River Shape, Not Just the Tile

Several reviewed hands emphasize LuckyJ's sensitivity to discard patterns and calls. It may push into one open hand, match-discard live honors early, or stop a seemingly ordinary tile when a river looks abnormal. This is why a static danger chart is not enough; the same 6m is different against a normal river, a honitsu river, and a riichi declaration sequence.

Last-Place Attack Still Has Brakes

When fighting to escape fourth, LuckyJ uses riichi pressure, calls for keiten, and hand-value upgrades more aggressively. But the review hands also show it refusing calls or discards that leave no safe exit after the next dangerous draw. The lesson is not "push because last." It is "buy pressure when the opponent must respect your hand, but do not remove every escape hatch."

Third Row Is the Training Zone

The strongest warning is that LuckyJ can look less reliable in the third discard row: sometimes too ready to release tenpai equity, sometimes strained by multiple threats. Treat late endgame as a separate skill. If NAGA, Mortal, and your point calculation all disagree with LuckyJ late, do not force the LuckyJ move into your game.

Synthesis rule: LuckyJ's strange-looking tiles are usually not random. Ask which switch changed: value threshold, distance-safety tradeoff, bad-block quality, opponent river, number of threats, or placement pressure.
01

Start with the placement problem, not the hand problem.

Before choosing a discard, ask what the hand is worth in placement terms. The same 2,000-point hand can be valuable, irrelevant, or actively bad depending on round and score. In reviewed hands, LuckyJ often changes the whole plan because a cheap win no longer protects the target placement, while a mangan changes everything. In the NAGA dataset, LuckyJ's result edge came from conversion: top-half games had 3.09 wins and 0.75 deal-ins per hanchan, while bottom-half games had 1.39 wins and 1.48 deal-ins.

Rule: On turn one, name the hand's job: quick end, mangan chase, defend lead, escape last, or keep options. If your discard does not serve that job, it is probably a habit discard.
  • When leading, cheap wins are still useful, but cheap risky wins lose value.
  • When second in South rounds, ask whether a mangan meaningfully changes the final order.
  • When far behind, riichi pressure and keiten calls gain value, but safe tiles still matter as tools that let you push later.
02

Combine chances until the table forces a commitment.

A central LuckyJ habit is keeping tiles that serve more than one future. In bad or unclear hands, it often balances open tanyao, yakuhai, honitsu, chanta, chiitoi, dora use, and safety. This is not indecision. It is controlled ambiguity. The hand-review material repeatedly shows hands that shift from three routes, to two routes, to one route, to defense, based on new draws and opponent signals.

In the dataset, LuckyJ disagreed with NAGA most often early: 45.2% of early decisions, compared with 30.7% in the middle and 27.9% late. Those early deviations are where route-combining happens.

Rule: If a tile contributes to two of speed, value, and safety, it usually beats a tile that contributes to only one, even when the one-purpose tile has more raw speed.
03

Bad shapes favor opening, but only with a plan.

LuckyJ opens poor hands more readily than a textbook closed-hand player because bad shapes need more outside help. A hand with several bad blocks may never become a respectable riichi hand. Passing a useful pon can be equivalent to quietly folding on turn two. The practical point is direct: bad shapes increase the speed gain from opening.

This does not mean call everything. LuckyJ called in 35.8% of hands and averaged 5.65 calls per hanchan, but it also skipped NAGA-preferred calls 1,996 times. The skill is not "open more"; the skill is "open hands whose closed route is fake, while keeping enough defense to survive."

Call if most answers are yes

  • The hand has poor closed prospects.
  • The call creates yaku, tenpai, or a real one-away hand.
  • The hand has at least one safe tile or a way to discard safely after the call.
  • The value is enough for the score situation, or ending the hand is itself valuable.
04

Every open call increases the price of safe tiles.

The shorter your hand becomes, the fewer random safe tiles you will draw and keep. LuckyJ's open hands often look aggressive, but many are built around saved honors, genbutsu, or tiles safe against the main threat. This is the opposite of the common mistake: open, throw all safety away, then become forced to push a bad hand.

Rule: After your first call, revalue every honor and every visible safe tile upward. If your hand is open and slow without safe tiles, you have usually created the worst possible position.

The recurring open-hand theme is "rush while preparing to defend." The NAGA data supports the shape of that style: safer-than-NAGA and riskier-than-NAGA deviations were almost balanced overall, but middle and late safer deviations rose sharply when defense started to matter. The reviewed examples add a sharper split: LuckyJ often treats one open opponent as readable, but becomes much stricter when riichi or multiple live threats enter.

05

Throw dangerous floating tiles before they become your problem.

LuckyJ is unusually hostile to dangerous floaters in slow hands. It will cut a middle tile that has some raw speed if that tile is likely to become an unsafe discard later. This is one of the largest differences between strong digital play and simple tile-efficiency play.

The hand reviews add a second reason: the river can make a tile's future worse even before anyone declares riichi. Against an abnormal discard pattern, a floater that looks ordinary by suji logic may already be a tile LuckyJ wants out of the hand.

The book-data pass found that early NAGA mismatches often involved LuckyJ cutting a simple while NAGA preferred an outside tile. This happened 4,766 times in early mismatches, far more than the reverse pattern. That is the signature of keeping flexible outside/safety/yaku material and shedding dangerous simple floaters before danger arrives.

Rule: In a far-away hand, do not ask only "what accepts more tiles?" Ask "which leftover tile will I be forced to discard when the first riichi appears?"
06

Value planning starts while the hand is still far away.

LuckyJ often sacrifices early speed for value because early flexibility is cheap to spend. Once a hand reaches one-away, every lost route is expensive. The review material repeatedly warns against racing to a tenpai that cannot satisfy the placement target. If you need mangan, start building mangan while you still have optionality.

Good sacrifice

Cut a modestly useful tile to preserve honitsu, chiitoi, toitoi, dora use, or yakuhai.

Bad sacrifice

Cut speed for a decorative yaku that does not change placement or hand class.

Rule: If the likely fast hand is not worth winning, do not optimize toward it. Build toward the score class you actually need.

A practical threshold from hand review: as a child, be suspicious of routes that end in riichi-only bad shape unless the river or point situation makes the pressure valuable. As dealer, one-han good shape and two-han hands become more acceptable because the dealer turn itself has value.

07

Call for tempo, denial, and survival, not just hand completion.

LuckyJ calls to break ippatsu, to contest a riichi with a qualified hand, to deny opponents time, and to convert bad closed shapes into usable open hands. But it also refuses marginal calls that merely create a cheap exposed hand. In the data, call opportunities were common: 39,749 possible call spots, with 7,091 actual calls across all games.

08

Riichi is a conversion tool, not a purity test.

LuckyJ's first-place games had 2.30 riichi per hanchan; fourth-place games had 1.78. The difference is not enormous, but it points in the right direction: riichi is part of conversion. The reviewed hands also emphasize that first-move riichi can be strong even with modest value, while bad-shape, low-value tenpai can be unworthy against multiple threats.

Riichi when

  • You are first to apply meaningful pressure.
  • The hand changes placement or denies opponents a cheap path.
  • Your wait/value combination is strong enough to push future danger.

Do not autopilot riichi when

  • Two opponents are already live threats.
  • Your hand is bad-shape, low-value, and replaceable by defense.
  • Dama or open play preserves a better placement line.

Last-place-avoidance reviews sharpen this: a bad-shape riichi-only hand can still be correct when you are last, the wait is not dead by river texture, and the opponents are incentivized to fold to your pressure. The bad riichi is the one that wins little, scares nobody, and forces you to pass several future danger tiles.

09

Push-fold is recalculated every draw.

A LuckyJ hand can go from attack to full fold in one draw. The reviewed hands stress this repeatedly: opponent progress, number of threats, remaining dangerous tiles, and your future discard path can change the answer instantly. The mistake to avoid is deciding "this is my pushing hand" and then ignoring new evidence.

Rule: Do not ask "is my hand good?" Ask "what dangerous tiles must pass before this hand wins, and are they worth the placement gain?"

A three-han hand may push against one riichi and fold against two. A hand with two future dangerous discards is much weaker than the same current tenpai with no future landmines.

Add opponent type to that calculation. LuckyJ is willing to push or match-discard against a single open hand because calls reveal shape. A riichi, a honitsu-looking river, or two coordinated threats should raise the required hand class immediately.

10

Late hands tighten: fewer deviations, fewer bad moves.

LuckyJ's early mismatch rate was 45.2%; late mismatch rate was only 27.9%. Bad-rate also fell from 18.1% early to 6.6% late. This is what disciplined play looks like. Early turns are for strategic shape, route selection, and value planning. Late turns are for precise execution: take tenpai safely, fold cleanly, or push only hands that deserve it.

This does not mean LuckyJ's late game is beyond review. The hand-review material explicitly warns that third-row spots are where LuckyJ can look least human-copyable, especially around multiple threats and tenpai/noten equity. Use LuckyJ late as a question generator, not as an answer key.

Rule: The later the hand, the less you are allowed to speculate. If the hand has not become real by the middle of row two, begin pricing safety more heavily. If the hand is real, count noten payments and remaining safe draws before surrendering it.
11

Drawn-hand points are part of the attack plan.

LuckyJ gained tenpai/noten points in 40.6% of drawn hands. This is not accidental noise. Reviewed hands show LuckyJ folding while preserving an outside chance at tenpai, or taking tenpai with the safest discard. The practical lesson is that "defense" and "winning the draw" are not opposites.

Rule: When folding, keep the route that may still produce safe tenpai. Discard the safe tile that leaves the most harmless future draws.
12

Do not worship NAGA match rate.

NAGA is useful because it highlights decisions worth reviewing. But LuckyJ's score in this dataset was almost uncorrelated with NAGA rating or match rate. The lowest NAGA-match decile actually had a better average placement than the highest NAGA-match decile. The useful target is not exact match. The useful target is eliminating the truly bad class of disagreement while preserving strategic deviations that serve placement, safety, and value.

Rule: Review NAGA disagreements in three buckets: obvious blunder, strategic trade-off, and robot-style insight. Only the first bucket should be blindly removed.
13

Clean yakuhai when an open hand has not shown its yaku.

A call is not only speed. It is a clue. When another player opens with a sequence or a non-yakuhai pon, the hand may still be missing the one tile that makes it legal. Dragons, round wind, and that player's seat wind become more important after the call, not less. LuckyJ often cleans those tiles before the open hand can pair or pon them into a contract.

This is different from hoarding yakuhai for your own hand. If the tile is a singleton, your hand is not close, and an opponent has already shortened their hand without revealing tanyao, yakuhai, or another clear yaku, the tile has become an opponent-enabling liability. Removing it early can be better than keeping a slightly prettier shape and donating the open hand's missing yaku later.

Clean the yakuhai when

  • An opponent has opened but has not shown an obvious yaku.
  • The tile is a dragon, round wind, or their seat wind.
  • You hold it as a singleton and it is not central to your value plan.
  • Your hand is not ready to punish the open hand immediately.
Rule: After any opponent call, ask what yakuhai would make that hand legal. If you are holding one as loose material, price it as their future yaku before you price it as your harmless honor.
14

Keep genbutsu and suji tiles as timed exits.

A safe tile is not only a folding tile. It is a future choice. LuckyJ often keeps a tile that is already in another player's river, or a tile that is suji to an opponent's river, while cutting a more flexible-looking tile now. The point is timing: spend shape while the table is still playable, keep the defensive exit for the turn when riichi, a second call, or a bad draw changes the hand's job.

The distinction matters. Genbutsu is an exact river-safe tile against the player who discarded it. Suji is weaker: it depends on the opponent's river showing the three-away tile in the same suit. LuckyJ's retained suji tiles are not magic safety. They are lower-friction exits that let a hand continue one more turn without becoming forced to push a live middle tile.

Read the timing

  • Early: a kept river-safe tile is permission to delay commitment, not proof of folding.
  • Middle: retained genbutsu or suji is often the brake that lets a hand keep attacking selectively.
  • Late: the same tile becomes concrete currency for safe tenpai, clean fold, or one final push.
Rule: Before discarding a tile that is in an opponent river or suji to that river, ask what your next safe discard will be after the first real threat. If the answer is "nothing," LuckyJ usually prices that kept tile much higher than raw ukeire does.

Practice system

How to train this style

Turn-one job naming

Before every first discard, write one phrase: quick end, value target, open rush, safety hedge, or last-place attack.

Three-route bad-hand drill

For every bad hand, mark the best tile for tanyao, yakuhai, and safety. Discard the tile that contributes least across all three.

Dangerous floater audit

On turn five, identify the tile you will hate discarding into riichi. If your hand is slow, consider cutting it now.

Call contract

Every call must state its contract: tenpai, real one-away shape, yaku creation, ippatsu break, or denial. No contract, no call.

Opponent contract audit

After every opponent call, mark dragons, round wind, and that player's seat wind. If their yaku is not visible, clean loose yakuhai before it becomes their contract.

River-safe inventory

Before the middle row, mark every tile in your hand that is already in an opponent river or suji to it. Do not spend the last exit casually.

Future push path

Before pushing, name every dangerous tile that must pass before you win. If there are two or more, downgrade the hand sharply.

Draw-tenpai defense

When folding, prefer safe discards that preserve harmless tenpai routes. Count noten-bappu as a real objective.

Penchan autopsy

When you break a penchan, write why: bad riichi-only future, value route, safety route, or river danger. If none applies, keep the block.

Third-row replay

Review every third-row push/fold with NAGA, Mortal, and your own point calculation. This is where copying LuckyJ blindly is most dangerous.

Sources and limits

What the evidence can and cannot prove

The NAGA reports show what LuckyJ did and where NAGA models disagreed. They do not prove the counterfactual result of replacing LuckyJ's move with NAGA's move. Supporting hand-review material was used to convert the raw patterns into strategy points.
